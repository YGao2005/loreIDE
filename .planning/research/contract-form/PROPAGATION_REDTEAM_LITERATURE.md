# PROPAGATION.md — Literature Red-Team

**Reviewer:** Claude (adversarial literature review)
**Date:** 2026-04-24
**Verdict:** CONDITIONAL ACCEPT — architecture survives if four specific holes are patched. The separation-of-concerns move is correct, but the detection layer's correctness story is weaker than the doc presents, the journal is under-specified as an event-sourced log, and the reconciliation layer over-trusts the LLM on exactly the task where recent literature shows LLMs fail most reliably.

---

## 1. Verdict

I agree with RETHINK's direction but not with the document's confidence. Separation of detection / intent-capture / reconciliation is structurally sound — it mirrors the cleanest prior art (Datalog/DRed incremental-maintenance algorithms, Huggingface model cards + registries, event-sourced systems with distinct command and query paths). The owner's original per-edit hook is correctly killed. **However**, PROPAGATION.md treats the detection layer as "unconditional" and "deterministic" in ways that don't hold up against 40 years of materialised-view-maintenance literature, and it lands the hardest cognitive work (cross-level propagation via "Propagate from children") on the LLM layer most recently shown to systematically fail at exactly this class of task. The doc is right that Attacks 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 "die" at the detection/journal boundary — but it introduces new failure modes (cascading invalidation, non-self-maintainable rollups, LLM overcorrection in reconcile) that the attack list doesn't enumerate. For a 1-week hackathon demo, this is fixable with three surgical changes listed in §4. For v2, the journal needs event-sourcing hygiene it does not yet have.

## 2. Prior-art failure modes the authors likely missed

### 2.1 Non-self-maintainability of aggregate rollups (Gupta & Mumick 1995; Motik, Nenov, Horrocks 2019)

Incremental view maintenance distinguishes *self-maintainable* views (can be refreshed from the delta alone) from ones that require re-reading the base tables. `rollup_hash = sha256(concat(child.section_hashes))` **is** self-maintainable for the *hash* — recomputing from `rollup_inputs` gives the right answer. But the system is not using `rollup_hash` as the contract body; it's using it as a *staleness flag* whose user-facing semantic is "your upstream prose may now be wrong about its children." That semantic is **not** self-maintainable — deciding whether L2's Invariants section is still accurate depends on *what changed* in L3's Invariants, not merely *that* something changed. The recent paper *Incremental Maintenance of DatalogMTL Materialisations* (arXiv:2511.12169, 2025) shows that even a hash-style incremental algorithm "often significantly outperforms rematerialisation" yet still requires periodic re-materialisation to avoid accumulated error; the classical DRed result is that delete-rederive cycles over-invalidate in the presence of recursion. Our L0–L3 chain is recursive by construction (L1 depends on L2 depends on L3). **Consequence for us:** the `rollup_hash` mismatch is a necessary-but-not-sufficient signal. If a child's Examples section gets reworded without semantic change, every ancestor flags amber; if a child's Invariants section gets subtly inverted (e.g., "must" → "should"), the hash catches it but the reconciler cannot tell whether the change is semantic until it reads both versions — which the current architecture does not persist.

### 2.2 Cascading invalidation and the "amber flood" (Bazel, Nix, Turborepo engineering postmortems)

Bazel/Nix/Turborepo all implement exactly the dependency-hash shape the doc proposes, and all three have public postmortems on cache-poisoning / over-invalidation: a single leaf change near the root of the DAG flips every upstream hash, producing a cache-miss storm. The "small edit, large consequence" attack (#6) is acknowledged, but the doc's mitigation — "batching" 12 upstream nodes in one reconcile — assumes the user will actually reconcile all 12. In practice (see Lamb & Zacchiroli 2021, *Reproducible Builds*), even highly motivated users defer-and-suppress when invalidation fans out past ~5 nodes. **Consequence for us:** once the graph shows amber on 40% of upstream nodes after a routine refactor, reconcile fatigue sets in and the amber signal becomes indistinguishable from noise — i.e., the exact failure mode the "red vs amber" distinction was meant to prevent. The doc's Open Question 5 names this as a color-key-of-confusion; literature says it's more severe: it's a structural property of hash-DAG invalidation, not a visual-design issue.

### 2.3 LLM systematic overcorrection on requirement-conformance judgement (Jin & Chen 2026, *Are LLMs Reliable Code Reviewers?* — arXiv:2603.00539)

This paper is devastating for Layer 3 ("Propagate from children"). Finding: LLMs *systematically misclassify correct code as non-compliant* with natural-language specs, and **detailed prompts requiring explanations and proposed corrections raise misjudgment rates**. The "Propagate from children" prompt in Layer 3 is exactly this shape — it hands the LLM current upstream + cited child sections + journal context and asks for a proposed diff with rationale. Jin & Chen's finding predicts this will produce *more* spurious propagation than a simpler prompt would. Mittal et al. 2026 (*Comparing Developer and LLM Biases in Code Evaluation*, arXiv:2603.24586) reports the best models underperform humans by 12–23% and identifies 35 distinct alignment gaps. Stengg et al. 2025 (*GPT-5 for Code-change Impact Analysis*, arXiv:2512.19481) specifically tested SOTA models on the code-change-impact task — **both GPT-5 and GPT-5-mini performed poorly** even with parent commit tree + diff hunks. This is the closest analogue to our reconcile task in the current literature, and the result is: LLMs are not yet reliable consequence-propagators. **Consequence for us:** the user gesture "Propagate from children" will produce confidently-wrong diffs at a rate the doc does not budget for. The query-time annotation in Layer 4 is a good hedge, but it assumes downstream agents read and respect the staleness annotation, which Singh et al. 2025 (*Do Code Models Suffer from the Dunning-Kruger Effect?*, arXiv:2510.05457) directly argues they do not — model confidence is proportional to competence in a biased way, and unfamiliar contexts (exactly our case: agent reading a stale L1) produce *stronger* overconfidence.

### 2.4 Event-sourcing hygiene: schema evolution, idempotence, replay semantics (Fowler 2005; Young 2010; Kleppmann 2017)

`.propagation_journal` is event sourcing. Event-sourced systems that do not commit up front to (a) event schema versioning, (b) idempotence under replay, and (c) a clear compaction/snapshot strategy reliably rot. PROPAGATION.md's journal has a freeform `{ts, session_id, tool, file, affected_uuids, intent}` schema with no version field, no guarantee that appending the same event twice is safe (what happens when `git pull` merges two branches that both journaled the same hook event?), and an explicit "pruning is a Phase 10+ concern." Open Question 2 names the committed-to-git issue but frames it as a noise problem; the deeper issue is that `intent` is extracted from a transcript at hook-fire time and is therefore **not idempotent across replays**. If the journal is committed to git and the repo is rebased / squashed / cherry-picked, the journal's apparent event order will desync from the actual contract history — and there is no canonical way to rebuild. Kleppmann's framing is that the journal *must* be a source of truth for the state it records; ours is a lossy side-channel. **Consequence for us:** after two weeks of active development the journal is a pile of plausible-sounding intent strings whose ordering doesn't match git history, and the "last N journal entries affecting any cited child" prompt fragment in Layer 3 becomes a source of plausible-but-hallucinated historical rationale.

### 2.5 Agent drift and sycophancy propagation in multi-agent chains (Rath 2026 arXiv:2601.04170; Kasprova et al. 2026 arXiv:2604.02668)

The architecture has a multi-agent chain the doc does not name as such: (Session A edits L3 → detection flags L1 stale → Session B reconciles L1). Rath 2026 shows measurable behavioural degradation over extended interactions in LLM agent systems; Kasprova et al. 2026 shows sycophancy *propagates* through multi-agent discussions — agents presented with another agent's prior output are biased toward agreement. **Consequence for us:** Session B, presented with Session A's journaled intent, is biased toward confirming rather than contesting Session A's propagation logic. This compounds 2.3 — the reconcile step is systematically biased toward accepting the drift it's supposed to audit. Scenario B from the original attack list (fresh-session flip-flop) is real; its *opposite* is also real and arguably worse — a fresh session *conforming* to the prior session's claim regardless of whether the current state supports it.

## 3. Specific attacks grounded in prior art

### Attack L-1: Cascading amber storm after dependency bump

Developer runs `npm update` on a frontend workspace; 40 L4 files touched; their `code_hash` changes re-fire the watcher. Under the current Phase 7 watcher, only L3/L4 were drift-flagged. Under the proposed architecture, the L3 `section_hashes` that re-derive from `write_derived_contract` get new values (even when the L3 Intent is unchanged — see §2.1: the hash is strictly deterministic on bytes, not semantics). Every L2 citing any of those L3s goes amber. Every L1 citing any of those L2s goes amber. The demo graph shows amber on 60%+ of nodes within 30 seconds. Literature prediction (Bazel postmortems, §2.2): the user clicks "Accept as-is" on every amber node because reconcile-fatigue dominates; the system silently annotates false confidence at the L1/L0 layer exactly because the one-line justification requirement is too light a gate to withstand bulk click-through. **This is a demo-killer** if the demo repo (vercel/commerce) has any upstream seeding with dense L2→L3 citations.

### Attack L-2: Reconcile-induced regression (the Jin & Chen attack)

User clicks "Propagate from children" on a legitimately stale L2 whose L3s have slightly refined their invariants (no semantic change — just prose polish). Per Jin & Chen 2026 (§2.3), the LLM produces a proposed diff that *rewrites* the L2 Invariants to match the L3 prose verbatim, stripping higher-level abstraction. User approves (the diff looks reasonable in isolation). L2 now says "checkout_api must return 401 on expired tokens" instead of "checkout gracefully handles unauthenticated users" — a level-violation that breaks the five-level abstraction hierarchy. Subsequent `find_by_intent("unauthenticated user handling")` returns nothing because the L2 no longer talks at L2's level. **Repro sketch:** seed vercel/commerce L2 "Checkout Auth" with abstract language; run LLM-based rephrasing on L3 "checkout_api.ts"; click Propagate; observe L2 getting dragged down to L3's vocabulary.

### Attack L-3: Journal-replay desync after rebase

Developer has 5 days of accumulated `.propagation_journal` entries locally. They rebase their branch against main, which squashes a feature branch that included 12 hook-journaled edits. The post-rebase journal contains entries for contract UUIDs in an order that doesn't match the post-rebase git history (§2.4). A reconcile three days later pulls "the last N journal entries affecting cited child 11111111-..." — but those entries reference a session_id from a squashed commit that no longer exists, an `affected_uuids` list that may include UUIDs whose contracts were reverted in a later squashed commit, and `intent` strings whose transcript_path is dangling. The reconcile prompt hands the LLM a plausible but phantom rationale. **Repro sketch:** branch, edit 3 L3 contracts over 5 hook fires, `git rebase -i main` and squash, run reconcile on the upstream L2; observe prompt assembly quoting intent from a session that no longer appears in git log.

### Attack L-4: Multi-agent sycophancy loop

Monday: Session A weakens an L3 invariant; journal records "loosened validation per user request." Tuesday: detection fires, L2 goes amber. Wednesday: Session B (new session) is invoked to reconcile. Per §2.5, Session B reads the journaled intent and is biased toward confirming that the L2 *should* now be weaker. It produces a diff weakening L2. User approves. Thursday: L1's rollup now goes amber. Session C reconciles L1, reads Session A's + Session B's journaled intents, produces a diff weakening L1. The multi-session chain has propagated a single Monday loosening up three abstraction levels with zero counter-pressure. **Repro sketch:** deliberately weaken an invariant at L3; run three sequential reconcile passes with a fresh `claude -p` each time; observe monotonic weakening at each level.

### Attack L-5: Rebuilding from `.contracts/` produces different hashes than the journal claims

The doc's detection property ("works on `git pull`, `sed` edits, edits from another machine") assumes that `section_hashes` computed by the Rust canonical parser matches `section_hashes` computed by the TypeScript MCP writer. Open Question 1 acknowledges this explicitly; §2.4's idempotence problem compounds it. If the two parsers disagree on whitespace normalisation in even one edge case (e.g., trailing spaces after a `##` heading, CRLF vs LF on a Windows collaborator's checkout), every rebuild flags every node amber. This is the classic Bazel "cache entry is corrupt because the hash input leaks non-determinism" problem. **Repro sketch:** edit a sidecar .md file in VS Code on Windows, save, commit, pull on macOS, run cache rebuild; observe universal amber.

## 4. Recommended architecture changes

### v1 must-fix (before the PostToolUse hook ships in Phase 8)

1. **Add a monotonic `rollup_generation: u64` counter to every upstream contract.** Incremented on every reconcile action (propagate / accept-as-is / edit-manually). The journal records the generation at the time the intent was captured. Reconciliation prompts include the generation, enabling detection of concurrent reconciles (two sessions both click reconcile on the same upstream) without requiring distributed mutex infrastructure. This is the "database monotonic version" pattern from event sourcing, absent in the current proposal. Without it, Attack L-4 has no termination condition. Cost: one u64 per contract.

2. **Require a deterministic canonical-form normaliser for `section_hashes`, implemented in exactly one place.** Pick Rust-only or TypeScript-only and have the other side shell out to it; do not implement both and hope the round-trip tests cover every edge case. Open Question 1 proposes parallel implementations; literature (Reproducible Builds, §2.2) is emphatic this pattern rots. Normalise: trim trailing whitespace per line, force LF, unicode-NFC, canonical JSON-no-trailing-comma for fenced code blocks, strip zero-width chars. Without this, Attack L-5 fires on the first multi-OS collaborator.

3. **Downgrade "Propagate from children" to "Draft propagation for review."** The current three-action panel (Propagate / Accept / Edit) frames the LLM output as a propagation. Per Jin & Chen 2026 (§2.3) and Stengg 2025 (§2.3), the LLM output is best understood as *a candidate draft the user must counter-verify*. Concretely: rename the button, require the user to explicitly click-through the diff with a "these N lines do/don't preserve the original meaning" confirmation, and **never** auto-update `rollup_hash` when Propagate lands — the user must re-click Accept as the separate step. This kills Attack L-2's silent L2-dragging-down regression by forcing human friction at the semantic-judgement step where LLMs are most unreliable.

4. **Gate the amber visual on a fan-in threshold per L1 node.** If a single git operation flips the amber state on more than 3 children of an L1, suppress the L1 amber and show a repo-level banner: "40 contracts re-derived; 3 L2 + 1 L1 awaiting bulk reconcile." This converts Attack L-1's storm into a demo-survivable "one widget, one click." The doc already has the "batching" language; it needs to extend to the *visual* layer, not just the reconcile prompt layer.

### v2 concerns (after hackathon)

5. **Version the journal schema with a `journal_version: 1` header row per `.propagation_journal` file**, and commit to a compaction mechanism before the journal exceeds ~500 lines. Event-sourcing literature (Kleppmann 2017) is unambiguous that un-versioned append-only logs become a liability at exactly the scale where they would otherwise become valuable. For v2, introduce weekly-rolled snapshot files (`.propagation_journal.2026-W17.json`) with a checksum chain. Retain line-granular events for the current week.

6. **Formalise the anti-sycophancy protection for multi-session reconcile.** A reconcile prompt that includes "last N journal entries" should mark those entries as *prior hypotheses* rather than *ground truth*, and should explicitly prompt the LLM to consider the null case ("the upstream may be correct and no propagation is needed"). Kasprova et al. 2026 (§2.5) shows that explicit null-hypothesis prompting reduces sycophancy propagation by 10.5 points. This is a prompt-engineering fix, not an architecture change, but it needs to be written down so Phase 9's prompt polish doesn't accidentally re-introduce the sycophancy vector.

7. **Consider making `rollup_hash` a semantic hash rather than byte hash for v2.** Use a locally-cached embedding per section; the `rollup_hash` mismatches only when the embedding distance between old and new children exceeds a threshold. This addresses §2.1 directly (byte-hash mismatches are necessary but not sufficient for semantic staleness). Out of scope for v1 — the infra cost is real. But the owner should know this is the eventual direction, because byte-hash rollup will produce sustained false-positive amber that erodes user trust.

### What to *remove* from the current proposal

- The claim "detection is unconditional" overstates the case. It is unconditional *modulo* the canonical-parser problem (#2 above). Rewrite as "detection is deterministic given a canonical section normaliser."
- The framing "Attack 4/5/6/7/9 die here" is too strong. They *degrade to known, bounded failure modes*. Attack 6 becomes fan-in storms (bounded by #4 above); Attack 2 becomes reconcile-induced regression (bounded by #3); Attack 9 becomes rare-but-real multi-session sycophancy (bounded by #6). The architecture shifts the failure modes, doesn't eliminate them.

---

**Bottom line for 1-week hackathon MVP:** the architecture survives with fixes 1–4. Fixes 5–7 are v2. The single highest-risk item is #3 (downgrading "Propagate from children" to a draft-with-friction workflow) — if only one of the four v1 fixes lands, it should be that one, because Attack L-2 is the scenario most likely to show up on camera during the demo.
